Greenhouse gasses

Animal husbandry produces a significant amount of GHGs. Two of the greatest offenders on this front are the cattle and dairy sectors, which account for more than half of all GHG emissions from animal agriculture as a whole. With 44% of the total, methane is the GHG that is released the most frequently. Carbon dioxide and nitrous oxide make up approximately equally the remaining 56%.Cattle make about 65 percent of the contributions made by animal agriculture, including cattle raised for manure, as working animals, as well as cattle bred for dairy and meat. To further break this down, the industry as a whole emits around 20% of its GHG emissions from the production of dairy products and about 41% from the production of beef. These are then followed by pig meat, which is responsible for about 9% of emissions, buffalo milk and meat, which together account for 8%, chicken meat and eggs, which together account for another 8%, and finally, small ruminant milk and meat (such as sheep and goats’ milk and meat), which is responsible for 6% of the industry’s GHG emissions. Other birds raised for food, such as geese and ducks, and non-edible goods make up the remaining portion.

Land

The animal agriculture business still occupies a substantial amount of land for grazing cattle, cultivating feed for the animals, and storing waste, despite the prevalence of factory farms that cram thousands of animals into a limited amount of area. Large areas of land are needed for these operations, which frequently result in deforestation and environmental degradation and severe environmental catastrophe. Whether for raising farmed animals or cultivating their feed, animal agriculture uses up more than 80% of cropland. One of the factors causing deforestation is the desire for such a vast amount of land. According to estimates, one to two acres of land are removed per second. Animal agribusiness is thought to be to blame for up to 90% of the degradation now occurring in the Amazon rainforest, while it accounts for up to 80% globally. Land deterioration is also facilitated by animal agriculture. For instance, cattle bred for meat on public lands in the western United States graze on all the natural and indigenous grasses, harming native species and accelerating desertification. Large tracts of unsuitable desert land are created by the extensive livestock grazing, which causes the soil to become depleted of nutrients and water. Due to the severe stress it is put under, the cropland that is used to grow the corn and other commodities fed to farmed animals—particularly to cattle meant for meat during their last few months of life—also suffers. Monoculture, an intense agricultural technique in which one crop is cultivated year after year, requires the clearing and usage of large areas of land. The time and energy required by the soil to maintain its structure and nutrients are not provided by this type of crop development. Large quantities of fertilizers and pesticides are frequently sprayed to the fields to maintain high agricultural yields, further degrading the environment and hurting local species. 3. Polution

Thousands of animals reside on factory farms, and each one urinates and feces daily. Due to inadequate living circumstances, illness, or accident, many of the animals will pass away before being brought to be slaughtered. There are probably a number of enormous holes where their remains, together with whatever garbage they may have produced, will be kept. These pits’ hazardous contents may seep into the groundwater or may be discharged during a flood, washing onto the yards or even the homes of neighboring homeowners. In addition to the risk posed by these cesspits, the towns where these farms are located also have to contend with the obnoxious noise and foul stench caused by thousands of animals being lined up to die. Due to the urgent demand for animal feed, which is generated for livestock, heavy dosages of fertilizers, antibiotics, and pesticides are frequently dumped into the environment. Dead zones, or parts of the local ecosystem that are utterly devoid of life, are produced by this surplus trash. There are presently 405 dead zones documented in the world, and decades of agricultural pollution have rendered more than half of America’s rivers and streams lifeless. Thankfully, research indicates that dead zones may be eliminated or much reduced if animal husbandry businesses clean up after themselves.

Water

Animal husbandry uses one-third of all drinking water despite the fact that millions of people lack access to safe drinking water. It is astounding how much water is needed to consume just one serving of an animal product. A pound of beef may need up to 1800 gallons of water during manufacturing, compared to just 53 gallons needed to create one egg. Popular plant-based protein source soy uses 71% less water than chicken does to create a pound of soy. The majority of the fresh water used in agriculture—largely for animal agriculture—occurs worldwide. In contrast, pulses have a two to six times smaller water impact than animal products. Although this figure does not include the irrigation and other water needs required to grow crops for animal feed, the livestock industry in the United States uses around 2 billion gallons of water every day. Every day, the aquaculture industry uses around 7.6 billion gallons. There are currently 405 identified dead zones around the world, and more than half of America’s rivers and streams are now unable to support life after decades of agricultural pollution. While some people believe that eating wild fish is more “natural” than eating fish from intense fisheries, the 300,000 whales, dolphins, and porpoises who are killed each year as a result of getting trapped in fishing nets are anything but natural. Thousands of turtles, seals, and birds also perish from wounds brought on by nets and tackle, and even while technology is progressing to prevent this “by-catch,” the industry is still doing a great deal of harm to an ecosystem that is already vulnerable.

Wildlife and biodiversity

According to a Chatham House analysis, species of bacteria, fungus, plants, and animals are disappearing at a faster rate than they have in the previous 10 million years. The conversion of lands once used for natural ecosystems into intensive, industrial-style agricultural is the primary cause of this biodiversity loss. Over 75 million hectares (approximately 1.5 times the size of Spain) of the Amazon rainforest were removed between 1978 and 2020 to create room for cattle rearing. The primary cause of biodiversity loss is a global food system that places a premium on inexpensive food and uses intensive agricultural practices that harm “soils and ecosystems.”
